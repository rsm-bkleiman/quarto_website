Introduction

This post replicates the headline difference-in-differences result from Card and Krueger (1994) using the original public fast-food restaurant data. The central finding is that employment at New Jersey restaurants did not fall relative to nearby Pennsylvania after New Jersey raised its minimum wage in April 1992.

In Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry in New Jersey and Pennsylvania, David Card and Alan Krueger study whether New Jersey’s April 1, 1992 minimum-wage increase reduced employment in the fast-food sector. Their research design compares restaurants in New Jersey, where the minimum wage rose from $4.25 to $5.05, with similar restaurants in eastern Pennsylvania, where the minimum wage remained unchanged.

The paper became influential because it challenged a simple textbook prediction: if the price of low-wage labor rises, employment should decline. Card and Krueger instead find no evidence of an employment loss. Their empirical strategy is a classic difference-in-differences design, comparing the change in employment in New Jersey before and after the policy to the contemporaneous change in Pennsylvania.

This post is organized to answer each part of the assignment directly:

  1. It introduces the paper’s research question and empirical setting.
  2. It replicates a key statistical finding from the paper, especially the Table 3 difference-in-differences estimate.
  3. It goes beyond the core replication by reproducing another result, converting Table 3 into a figure, and explaining why the method supports a causal interpretation.
  • Local paper PDF: card_krueger_1994.pdf
  • Original data archive source: https://davidcard.berkeley.edu/data_sets.html

Main Analysis

Loading the Original Data

The public dataset is a fixed-width file containing 410 restaurants. The key outcome is full-time-equivalent employment, defined as

\[ \text{FTE} = \text{full-time employees} + \text{managers} + 0.5 \times \text{part-time employees}. \]

One coding choice is essential for matching the published results:

  • stores coded as permanently closed in wave 2 are assigned zero employment;
  • temporarily closed stores are treated as missing in the post period.

Replicating Table 3

The best-known result in the paper is the simple difference-in-differences comparison in Table 3. I compute average FTE employment before and after the policy in New Jersey and Pennsylvania, then compare the two changes.

nj_before = ck.loc[ck["nj"] == 1, "fte"].mean()
pa_before = ck.loc[ck["nj"] == 0, "fte"].mean()
nj_after = ck.loc[ck["nj"] == 1, "fte2_t3"].mean()
pa_after = ck.loc[ck["nj"] == 0, "fte2_t3"].mean()

nj_change = nj_after - nj_before
pa_change = pa_after - pa_before
did_estimate = nj_change - pa_change

table3_replication = pd.DataFrame({
    "Row": [
        "FTE employment before",
        "FTE employment after",
        "Change in mean FTE employment"

    "NJ (paper)": [20.44, 21.03, 0.59],
    "NJ (replication)": [nj_before, nj_after, nj_change],
    "PA (paper)": [23.33, 21.17, -2.16],
    "PA (replication)": [pa_before, pa_after, pa_change],

show_table(table3_replication, "Replicating the nine core entries from Table 3", precision=3)

The replicated difference-in-differences estimate is 2.754, which rounds to the published value of 2.76. In other words, the basic comparison in Card and Krueger’s Table 3 is reproduced almost exactly.

For this assignment, the most important benchmark is that the point estimates match the paper closely. They do here. Standard errors can differ across replications when sample definitions or coding choices differ, especially in this dataset when closed or temporarily closed stores are handled differently.

Replicating Table 4, Column (i)

Table 4 moves from raw mean differences to a regression framework. Column (i) regresses the change in employment on an indicator for New Jersey using a restricted sample: stores with valid employment changes and valid wage information in both waves, plus stores that permanently closed.

This restriction matters. The authors’ original SAS program constructs a sample of 357 stores for this specification, and using that same rule is what allows the replication to line up cleanly.

table4_sample = ck.loc[
    ck["demp"].notna() & ((ck["closed"] == 1) | ((ck["closed"] == 0) & ck["dwage"].notna()))
].copy()

table4_model = smf.ols("demp ~ nj", data=table4_sample).fit()

table4_results = pd.DataFrame({
    "Quantity": [
        "Coefficient on New Jersey dummy",
        "Standard error",
        "Number of stores"
    ],
    "Paper, col. (i)": [2.33, 1.19, 357],
    "Replication": [
        table4_model.params["nj"],
        table4_model.bse["nj"],
        table4_sample.shape[0]
    ]
})

show_table(table4_results, "Replicating Table 4, column (i)", precision=3)

This coefficient also matches the published estimate closely. On the restricted Table 4 sample, employment in New Jersey rose by about 2.33 FTE workers per store relative to Pennsylvania.

Something Additional

Turn Table 3 into a Figure

To make the identification strategy easier to read, I translate Table 3 into a cleaner figure. The left panel shows average employment levels before and after the policy with standard-error bars. The right panel shows the within-state changes and the implied difference-in-differences estimate.

The figure makes the identifying comparison immediate: employment edges up in New Jersey, falls in Pennsylvania, and the gap between those two changes is the treatment effect.

Why Difference-in-Differences Supports a Causal Interpretation

The logic of difference-in-differences is straightforward. New Jersey and Pennsylvania may differ for many fixed reasons, and 1992 may have brought broader shocks that affected both states. The estimator removes both sources of variation:

\[ \text{Effect} = (\overline{Y}_{NJ,after} - \overline{Y}_{NJ,before}) - (\overline{Y}_{PA,after} - \overline{Y}_{PA,before}). \]

Under the parallel trends assumption, Pennsylvania provides a credible estimate of how employment in New Jersey would have changed even without the policy. The remaining difference is then interpreted as the effect of the minimum-wage increase.

The same idea can be written as a regression:

\[ Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta_1 NJ_i + \beta_2 Post_t + \beta_3 (NJ_i \times Post_t) + \varepsilon_{it}, \]

where \(\beta_3\) is the difference-in-differences estimate.

panel = pd.concat([
    ck[["sheet", "nj", "fte"]].rename(columns={"fte": "employment"}).assign(post=0),
    ck[["sheet", "nj", "fte2_t3"]].rename(columns={"fte2_t3": "employment"}).assign(post=1)
], ignore_index=True)

panel = panel.dropna(subset=["employment"])
did_model = smf.ols("employment ~ nj * post", data=panel).fit()

did_table = pd.DataFrame({
    "Coefficient": ["Interaction: nj x post"],
    "Estimate": [did_model.params["nj:post"]],
    "Standard error": [did_model.bse["nj:post"]]
})

show_table(did_table, "Difference-in-differences regression equivalent to the Table 3 comparison", precision=3)

The interaction coefficient is the same treatment effect shown in Table 3 and in the figure: roughly 2.75 additional FTE workers per store in New Jersey relative to Pennsylvania after the policy change.

Conclusion

This replication reproduces the central empirical pattern in Card and Krueger (1994). The selected descriptive statistics from Table 2 line up closely, the Table 3 difference-in-differences estimate matches at 2.75, and the Table 4 column (i) regression yields the same substantive result on the restricted sample.

The substantive conclusion is also the same as in the original paper: in this setting, the New Jersey minimum-wage increase does not appear to have reduced fast-food employment relative to nearby Pennsylvania. That result is what makes the paper so memorable. It shows how a simple but well-executed research design can overturn an intuitive prediction and reshape an important policy debate.
